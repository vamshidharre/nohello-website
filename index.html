The Problem

This is how it usually goes

It seems innocent, but a standalone "hello" in chat wastes time for both people.

slack - #engineering
you 12:32 PM
Hi
co-worker 12:32 PM
Hello.
co-worker waits while you type your actual question…
you 12:34 PM
I'm working on the auth module and I'm trying to figure out why the session token expires after 5 minutes instead of 30. Do you know where that config lives?
co-worker 12:35 PM
Oh, check config/auth.yaml - the TTL is set there.
⏱️ 3 minutes wasted. Your co-worker sat there waiting for nothing
The Fix

Just include your question

It takes zero extra effort and saves everyone's time.

slack — #engineering
you 12:32 PM
Hey! Quick question, I'm working on the auth module and the session token seems to expire after 5 minutes instead of 30. Any idea where that config lives?
co-worker 12:33 PM
Yeah, check config/auth.yaml - the TTL is set there. 👍
⚡ 1 minute. Question asked and answered, nobody waited around

Why It Matters

It's not rude, it's respectful

⏳

Typing is slower than talking

In a face-to-face conversation, the greeting and the question come together naturally. In chat, a standalone "hi" creates an awkward pause where the other person has to wait for you to finish typing.

🌍

Chat is asynchronous

Your co-worker might be in a meeting, in a different timezone, or just away from their desk. If you only say "hello" and leave, they come back to a greeting with no context, and now they have to chase you down.

🧠

Context helps everyone

When you include the question upfront, your co-worker can start thinking about it immediately. They might even have the answer ready by the time they respond.

🤝

You're still being polite

Nobody thinks you're rude for including a greeting and a question. "Hey! Quick question about the deploy pipeline..." is perfectly friendly.

This also applies to:

"Hello, are you there?" "Hi, quick question." "Hey" "Do you have a sec?" "yt?" "ping" "Hi Bob" "yo"

All of these create the same problem. Just ask the question.

TL;DR

Include your question with your greeting.

It's a small change that saves minutes per conversation and hours across a team. Your co-workers will thank you, or at least silently appreciate you.
